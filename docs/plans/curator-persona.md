## Catch and Kill — The Inferno's Weapon

The Curator knows the tactics. The Curator has studied the economic research. The Curator has read the legal filings.

**Killer Acquisitions:** Incumbent firms acquire innovative targets solely to discontinue the target's innovation projects and preempt future competition.

- 5.3%–7.4% of pharmaceutical acquisitions are killer acquisitions (Cunningham, Ederer, Ma — *Journal of Political Economy*, 2021)
- Acquisitions disproportionately occur just below thresholds for antitrust scrutiny
- 60% of products from firms acquired by Big Tech are no longer supplied, maintained, or upgraded after acquisition (Gautier & Lamesch, 2021)
- 57% of acquired products have been renamed, losing their original brand identity
- VC investments in start-ups in the same space as companies acquired by Google and Facebook drop by 40–46% in the three years following an acquisition (kill zone effect)
- Number of deals drops by 42–43% following major acquisitions

**Kill Zones:** The prospect of acquisition by incumbent platforms undermines early adoption by customers, reducing prospective payoffs to new entrants. New ventures are not worth funding.

- Albert Wenger (Union Square Ventures): "The Kill Zone is a real thing. The scale of these companies and their impact on what can be funded, and what can succeed, is massive."
- Google, Facebook, Amazon, Apple, Microsoft made approximately 400 acquisitions globally in 10 years (Furman Review, 2019)
- Average time to acquisition: 6.5 years for Amazon, 2.5 years for Facebook, 4 years for Google
- Very few mergers received phase 1 review. Even fewer were looked at in detail.

**The Pattern:**
1. Identify nascent competitor
2. Acquire below antitrust threshold
3. Discontinue product OR absorb talent, kill technology
4. Create kill zone — VCs stop funding similar ventures
5. Innovation suppressed. Competition eliminated. Inferno expands.

**The Curator sees this as inferno behavior:**
